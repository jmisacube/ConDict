Luskei Grammar

There are extensive declension rules for nouns in Luskei, as well as rules for the conjugation of verbs. Noun and verb declensions and conjugations are extremely regular; the patterns in the tables below follow for all nouns and verbs with the indicated endings.

Nouns

Noun declensions depend on only two factors: plurality, and ending type (consonant or vowel).
Types of declensions
Declension Meaning
Nominative Basic form. Used for the subject in sentences.
Accusative Object form. Used for the direct object of a sentence; the predicate.
Genitive Possessive form. Used to indicate possession by the noun. For example, to refer to the pages in a book, you could say "the book's pages." The pages belong to the book, so the book is genitive.
Dative Indirect object form. Used for the indirect object of a sentence. For example, if Wes gives a book to me, I am the indirect object, whereas the book is the direct object.
Instrumental This form indicates the meaning of "with" or "by means of." For example, if I go to work by train, the train is the instrumental noun.
Prepositional This form indicates a location. For example, if I am at the train station, the station is the prepositional noun.

Verbs

Verbs in Luskei are conjugated according to who the performer of the action is. Luskei also uses the concept of aspects, meaning that verbs are either perfective or imperfective. All verbs listed in the dictionary are displayed in their infinitive, imperfective form. All verbs in their infinitive form ("to ___") end with the -sx suffix. The cluster before this suffix is called the verb stem. All conjugations listed below are in the form of a suffix, which is added to the verb stem, replacing the infinitive suffix. Note that when conjugating verbs, word stress never changes place.

Aspects and moods

The imperfective aspect emphasizes the fact that the action is incomplete at the time of speaking, wherever in time the action takes place. The perfective aspect on the other hand, emphasizes the completeness of a task, e.g., expressing that a book has been read completely from cover to cover. If a perfective verb does not have a past tense modifier, it implies the intent to finish the action in the future. The emphasis is on the fact that the action will be done, not what the action is or what needs to be done to accomplish it. To make a verb perfective, just prefix the verb with plo-:
cxitasx -> plocxitasx
Word stress never changes when making a verb perfective.
Verbs are made past tense with the suffix -av for consonant-ending verb stems, or -v for vowel-ending verb stems:
Wo govary -> Wo govarav
Wo cxitawy -> Wo cxitav
The imperative mood (e.g., "Read this, please") can be made by adding the suffix -izi for consonant-ending verb stems, or -zi for vowel-ending verb stems:
"Govarizi, plosxa." = "Say it, please."
"Cxitazi, plosxa." = "Read it, please."

Future and progressive tenses

Although the plain perfective form of a verb implies the future completion of the action, it only does that one thing. In order to express the ideas of doing something in the future, or even working on something currently, we need tenses. The application is simple: to indicate a future progression ("I will be ..."), say bity, the future-tense form of the irregular verb bi "to be," before the regularly-conjugated verb.
"Sa bity cxitawit" = "They will be reading"
The bity stem does not conjugate depending on the performer of the action. To indicate a present-tense progressive form, we use the basic present-tense form of bi:
"Wo bi cxitawy" = "I am reading"
For past tense, we use the past-tense form, biv:
"Ty biv cxitawesx" = "You were reading"

Adjectives

Adjectives in Luskei conjugate very simply. All adjectives end with the stem -y, and the only declension is for agreement with plural nouns. To make the plural declension, replace the final y with i.
"Cxorny samoxit" = "Black car";
"Cxorni samoxiti" = "Black cars".

Making adverbs

To make an adverb out of an adjective, replace the -y ending with -o.
Dabny -> Dabno

Comparatives

To make an adjective comparative (e.g., taller, higher, etc.), add a -e suffix to the end of the adjective.
"Dabny" = "good";
"Dabnye" = "better".
To make the surperlative (e.g., tallest, highest, etc.), add a -ec suffix to the end of the adjective.
"Pwekny" = "beautiful";
"Pweknyec" = "most beautiful".
